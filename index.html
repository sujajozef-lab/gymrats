Foundation

Before you
begin

These eight dates work best when both partners enter them as curious explorers — not debaters or problem-solvers. The goal is understanding, not agreement. Space dates at least a week apart and never skip ahead.

Environment
  • Choose a private place — no busy restaurants for sensitive topics
  • Phones face-down, notifications off, for the whole date
  • Aim for 1–2 hours minimum — these conversations need room
  • Pick a time when neither of you is exhausted
Emotional prep
  • Take 10 minutes alone to decompress before you sit down
  • Remind yourself: your partner is not your opponent
  • Come with curiosity — assume there's something you don't yet know
  • Set a shared intention aloud before you start
Ground rules
  • One person speaks at a time — no interrupting, even to agree
  • Use "I feel…" language; avoid "You always…" or "You never…"
  • Agree on a safe word to pause without it feeling like abandonment
  • You can pass on any question — nothing is mandatory
At the start of every date
  • Begin with one genuine appreciation of your partner
  • Check in on energy: "How are you feeling going into this?"
  • Remind yourselves you're on the same team
  • Read the date topic together first

Do

  • Listen to understand, not to respond
  • Ask follow-ups: "Tell me more about that"
  • Validate feelings even when you disagree with them
  • Acknowledge when something is new information to you
  • Express gratitude at the end, even if it was hard

Don't

  • Turn the date into a therapy session or intervention
  • Give advice when your partner only needs to be heard
  • Bring up unrelated grievances mid-conversation
  • Check your phone, even once
  • Push through emotional flooding — always honour the pause

Trust & Commitment

The foundation of everything else

Explore what trust and commitment mean to each of you — not just fidelity, but emotional availability. Ask: "In what ways have I shown up for you? Where have I fallen short?"

Key steps

  • Share one moment when you felt truly safe with your partner
  • Define commitment beyond loyalty — presence, honesty, consistency
  • Ask about hidden fears: "What could destroy our relationship?"
  • Make a shared promise about handling future breaches of trust
  • Acknowledge past moments where trust was tested

Questions to ask

  • When do you feel most secure — and most uncertain — in our relationship?
  • Have I done something that damaged your trust, even in a small way?
  • What does loyalty mean to you beyond being faithful?
  • What would you need from me to feel completely safe?
  • If our relationship were to end, what would you say broke down first?

Step back if...

  • The conversation turns into blame — trust is built here, not prosecuted
  • Either partner becomes defensive — take a 20-minute break
  • You feel pressure to resolve everything — naming the issue is enough
Opening ritual · 5 min
The gratitude exchange
Trust grows in small moments of recognition. This anchors you in what already exists before exploring what needs attention.
1
Sit facing each other. Take one slow breath together.
2
Each share one specific thing the other did recently that made you feel safe — be precise, not vague.
3
The listener responds only with: "Thank you for seeing that." Nothing more.
4
Switch. Repeat.
5
Close together: "I'm here, and I'm not going anywhere."
Why it works: Naming specific acts of care activates the brain's safety response — the ideal state for a trust conversation.

02

Conflict

Fighting smarter, not harder

Understand your conflict patterns — not to solve every argument, but to learn how you each handle tension. The goal is curiosity, not winning.

Key steps

  • Identify your conflict style: escalate, withdraw, or deflect?
  • Share a recurring argument — describe your feeling, not their fault
  • Find the hidden dream beneath the repeated fight
  • Agree on a repair signal for when things escalate
  • Discuss what a fair fight looks like to both of you

Questions to ask

  • What do I do when we argue that hurts you the most?
  • What are you really asking for when we fight about that topic?
  • How did conflict work in your family growing up?
  • What would make you feel heard during a disagreement?
  • Is there a fight we've had that still sits with you — unresolved?

Step back if...

  • You feel flooded — heart racing, mind blank. You cannot listen in that state
  • You're revisiting actual argument content — focus on patterns, not incidents
  • One person starts listing grievances — this is not a complaint session
Opening ritual · 5 min
The temperature check
Before talking about conflict, know where you each are. This exercise normalises honesty about your starting state.
1
Each rate how you're feeling right now: 1 (calm) to 10 (already tense).
2
Share your number first — no explanation yet. Just the number.
3
One sentence each on what's behind it — stress, tiredness, nerves, anything.
4
If either is above 7, wait 15 minutes and do something calming first.
5
If both are under 7, say together: "Tonight we're curious, not combative."
Why it works: Checking in first prevents you carrying hidden tension into the conversation and mistaking it for conflict with each other.

Sex & Intimacy

Desire, closeness, and vulnerability

Talk about what intimacy means beyond the physical — safety, desire, and emotional closeness are all part of this. Approach with warmth and no pressure to perform.

Key steps

  • Share what makes you feel most desired and emotionally connected
  • Talk about the difference between sex and intimacy for each of you
  • Discuss how stress and life changes affect your desire
  • Ask what you could do more of — and what you'd like to change
  • Explore what novelty or spontaneity means to each of you

Questions to ask

  • When do you feel closest to me — physical intimacy, or at other moments?
  • Is there something about our sex life you've wanted to say but haven't?
  • How do you feel about how often we are intimate right now?
  • When I initiate, do you feel desired or pressured?
  • What would make you feel more emotionally connected before being physical?

Step back if...

  • The talk drifts into criticism of sexual performance — it shuts people down
  • You're comparing to early dating — it pathologises normal evolution
  • Shame enters the room — no one should feel judged for their needs
Opening ritual · 3–4 min
The two-minute hold
Physical closeness before talking about intimacy is not a contradiction — it's the point. Move from heads to bodies before words.
1
Stand up and hold each other — a real, unhurried hug. Not a greeting. A staying hug.
2
Hold for at least two minutes. No talking.
3
Notice: your breathing, the warmth, any tension in your own body.
4
When you separate, each share one word for how you feel right now.
5
Sit down. Begin the date from that place.
Why it works: A sustained hold triggers oxytocin, reduces cortisol, and creates felt connection before the vulnerability of this conversation begins.

Work & Money

Values, security, and shared futures

Money is rarely about money — it's about security, freedom, control, and values. Understand your partner's financial personality, not just their spending habits.

Key steps

  • Share your earliest money memory and how it shaped your beliefs
  • Identify your money values: security, freedom, generosity, status?
  • Discuss what "enough" means to each of you
  • Talk about work — do you feel seen and supported in your career?
  • Build a shared vision of financial security 5–10 years from now

Questions to ask

  • Was money a source of stress, safety, or something else growing up?
  • Do you feel financially secure in our relationship right now?
  • What does financial freedom look like to you?
  • Is there a financial decision I've made that worried or frustrated you?
  • Do you feel supported in your career goals — or held back?

Step back if...

  • The conversation becomes about fault for past financial mistakes
  • You start negotiating a budget — that's practical, not emotional
  • Resentment about income inequality is triggered — it needs its own space
Opening ritual · 8 min
The money memory
Money conversations hurt when they're about numbers. This ritual grounds you in the emotional history each person brings to the topic.
1
Each take two minutes in silence to recall your earliest memory involving money.
2
Share the memory in full. The listener only listens — no commentary yet.
3
After both share, each answer: "What feeling does that memory leave you with?"
4
Acknowledge what you heard: "It makes sense that you think about money the way you do."
5
Only then begin the date.
Why it works: Your financial personality was shaped long before you met your partner. Understanding its roots prevents you from taking each other's reactions personally.

05

Family

Roots, roles, and the families we came from

You didn't just marry a person — you married their entire family history. Explore how each of your upbringings shape your expectations of partnership and parenting.

Key steps

  • Describe your family growing up — what was the emotional climate?
  • Identify which patterns you want to carry forward and which to leave behind
  • Discuss roles: who did what, and what do you expect now?
  • If you have or want children, explore parenting philosophies and fears
  • Ask what your parents got right — and what you wish had been different

Questions to ask

  • What's one thing from your upbringing you want us to carry forward?
  • What pattern from your family are you afraid of repeating?
  • How was love expressed in your home — words, acts, time?
  • What did conflict look like in your household — resolved or buried?
  • What kind of family culture do you want us to create together?

Step back if...

  • The conversation indicts each other's families — they aren't the enemy
  • Childhood trauma surfaces unexpectedly — it deserves a supported space
  • In-law tensions dominate — focus on values and patterns, not specific people
Opening ritual · 7–8 min
Show me one thing
Family is abstract until it's specific. Bring your origins into the room concretely before you discuss them.
1
Before the date, each find one photo or small object connected to your family or childhood.
2
Share it with your partner. Tell the story behind it in two or three minutes.
3
The listener asks just one follow-up: "What do you love most about that memory?"
4
Put the objects somewhere visible for the rest of the date.
5
Begin, knowing you've each brought your family into the room with you.
Why it works: Objects bypass defensiveness. They make family feel present and real — not a concept to debate, but a life to understand.

Play & Adventure

Fun, spontaneity, and shared joy

Couples who play together stay together. Explore what lights you both up and how well you know each other's idea of fun.

Key steps

  • Share your happiest childhood memory of play or adventure
  • Describe your dream adventure together — big or small
  • Identify activities that energise you vs. ones you do out of obligation
  • Find at least one new thing you'd both like to try
  • Talk about what "fun" looked like early in your relationship

Questions to ask

  • What playful thing we used to do have we stopped — and do you miss it?
  • What would your ideal spontaneous day with me look like?
  • Is there something you secretly want to try but haven't mentioned?
  • Do we laugh enough together? What makes you laugh with me?
  • What does adventure mean to you — travel, novelty, risk, something else?

Step back if...

  • It becomes a catalogue of what's missing or boring in your relationship
  • Your partner's idea of fun is dismissed as childish — all play is valid
  • Comparison to other couples creeps in — it leads nowhere useful
Opening ritual · 3 min
The 60-second silly
You cannot talk about play while being serious. This cracks open lightness before the date begins.
1
Set a 60-second timer.
2
Both must make each other laugh before it runs out — using only face, voice, or body. No tickling. No props.
3
If the first person fails, the other tries.
4
Sit down still smiling.
5
Begin by sharing: "The funniest thing we ever did together was…"
Why it works: Laughter is physiologically incompatible with anxiety. Starting from joy makes it far easier to talk honestly about fun.

Growth & Spirituality

Meaning, purpose, and personal evolution

The deepest questions — what do you believe, what gives your life meaning, and how do you grow together? There are no right answers, only honest ones.

Key steps

  • Share what gives your life a sense of meaning and purpose
  • Discuss your beliefs — spiritual, philosophical, or secular
  • Ask: "How have you changed in the last five years? What changed you?"
  • Explore what personal growth looks like and how you support each other in it
  • Discuss rituals or practices that feel meaningful to you as a couple

Questions to ask

  • What do you believe happens after we die — and does it bring you comfort?
  • What gives your life the most meaning right now?
  • Do you feel I support who you're becoming, or who you used to be?
  • Is there a part of yourself you feel you've lost in our relationship? Or gained?
  • What would you regret not doing if you knew your time was limited?

Step back if...

  • It turns into a debate about whose beliefs are correct — understand, don't convert
  • Existential fears become overwhelming — that's deep work, not a dinner topic
  • One partner feels pressured to adopt the other's worldview
Opening ritual · 8–10 min
The silence sit
This date asks the biggest questions. It deserves the most intentional opening. Shared silence is not awkward — it is the beginning of the conversation.
1
Sit together somewhere quiet. Turn off all sound.
2
Set a timer for five minutes. Sit in complete silence side by side or facing each other.
3
No phones. No fidgeting. Just sit.
4
When the timer ends, each share the first word that came to mind during the silence.
5
Use those two words as the opening question: "Why did that word come to you?"
Why it works: Silence creates interiority. Meaning and purpose live beneath the noise — five minutes shared quiet is often more connecting than five minutes of conversation.

Dreams & Visions

The future you're building together

The capstone date. Build a shared map of the future — not a rigid plan, but a living vision that honours both people's dreams.

Key steps

  • Each share your deepest personal dream — something never fully voiced
  • Describe your ideal life together in ten years
  • Look for overlap and divergence — where do dreams align and pull apart?
  • Discuss what legacy you want to leave as a couple and as individuals
  • Create a shared vision statement: "We are building a life that…"

Questions to ask

  • What's a dream you've had since childhood you've never told me about?
  • If money and fear were removed, what would you do with your life?
  • What does success look like to you — and does our life reflect that?
  • What do you want people to say about us as a couple when we're gone?
  • Is there a dream you're afraid to share because you think I won't support it?

Step back if...

  • Dreams are met with "that's not realistic" — it kills vulnerability instantly
  • Diverging dreams are treated as incompatibility — different visions can coexist
  • Anxiety about the future overrides possibility — return to what's already good
Opening ritual · 10 min
The future letter
This is the last and biggest date. The ritual opens you to a future orientation — from who you've been to who you are becoming.
1
Each take a piece of paper and write a letter to yourself, ten years from now. Five to eight sentences. What do you hope is true?
2
Take five minutes. Write in silence.
3
Read your letters aloud to each other. No interruption.
4
Each respond with: "The part that moved me most was…"
5
Fold the letters and keep them. Begin the date knowing you've spoken your future into the room.
Why it works: Writing activates future-self thinking, which creates hope and intentionality — exactly the mindset needed to build a shared vision rather than manage current problems.
